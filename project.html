What effect has increased commercialisation had on the Premier League and European Football?

Aims:

Sport is no longer simply the form of entertainment we once knew it to be, it has now developed to be a global industry drawing attraction from all areas and individuals. As the highest viewed sport internationally, football is at the forefront of this evolution, displaying substantial change in the sport’s motive of the past, present and future. The observations and analysis of this page should help us to understand how individual stakeholders’ interests; for example, players, teams, leagues and fans, have been affected by the growing commercialisation of the sport, focussing primarily on England’s topflight and Europe’s most prestige competition. Commercialisation will focus on the ways that the sport is projected as a commodity with the purpose of increasing cash flow and investment. This will allow us to determine which parties have seen the drastic changes, both positive and negative, or perhaps what areas of the game more resources are being distributed to.

Challenges:

Unlike readily available macroeconomic data, most information relating to my question is not situated on worldwide databases, and often a single source may supply one unit of data. Similarly, the evidence was often located within html rather than in a data frame already.

A significant struggle when retrieving data was PDF files, given most financial figures were embedded into reports. Persistent attempts to scrape these sources were unsuccessful, leading to timely manual processing and reduced automation. Similarly, the desired information didn’t align with many APIs, either requiring purchasing or displaying irrelevant data on regularly updated scores or statistics.

However, where possible I have scraped data in Colaboratory. Focussing on the page source and split functions from pandas, initial struggles disappeared and creating clean data frames became easier. Most sources display discrete values for a single season or team, therefore running the code consistently was unnecessary.

Creating a responsive page also generated issues, originally producing dimensionally different charts, although eventually the use of container elements for height and width whilst manipulating the CSS allowed for successful resizing.

Conclusion:

Firstly, it is important to stress that commercialisation of the sport, although often a criticised progression, does not have only negative effects on the sport. Globalisation is a primary reason, with the accessibility to broadcast the game and interact with new communities greater than it has ever been before, whilst the popularity across regions also grows. Simultaneously, as a fan, the quality of the sport has never reached the levels of the present; new technologies and innovations creating new possibilities.

On the other hand, the competitive nature at the pinnacle of the sport may be viewed as slightly diminished, as successful teams remain ahead of the competition. For the fan, there’s more to watch than before, yet this has come at a cost with significant subscription prices and ticket price jumps for most teams across the board, although this may not be visible for the lowest end tickets.

This analysis could continue to so many applicable areas, venturing to lower leagues and the rapidly inclining presence of women’s football. Sport has always been a fascinating spectacle, yet the story behind what happens on the pitch is something I hope to explore in the future.
